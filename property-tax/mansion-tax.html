New York Mansion Tax Overview

When purchasing residential real estate in NYC, buyers will likely be required to pay the city’s Mansion Tax, which is a one-time transfer tax that is levied on properties valued at $1 million or more. While the tax has been lauded as a source of much-needed funding for affordable housing initiatives, it has also come under fire as a deterrent to wealthy homebuyers and as an unfair burden on property owners. Fortunately, there are ways to avoid this tax and still get into your dream home.

In its current form, the Mansion Tax is a percentage of the purchase price of a property that begins at 1% for homes priced at $1 million and increases as the purchase price rises. The rate applies to both co-ops and condos, as well as single- and two-family homes. The tax is paid by the buyer at closing and funds are used to support a variety of affordable housing initiatives in the city.

While a 1% Mansion Tax may not seem like a large amount, it can add up quickly on high-value properties and is typically in addition to other transaction costs such as the RPTT. Therefore, it’s important to budget for this when purchasing property in NYC.

The Mansion Tax has had significant impacts on the luxury NYC real estate market, and some experts believe that it will continue to drive down sales of high-priced properties in the near future. In fact, the Mansion Tax has already been cited as a reason that some homebuyers are choosing to purchase homes outside of the city.

Aside from the potential impact on the luxury market, the Mansion Tax has a number of other consequences. For example, it is a significant expense for buyers and can impact their ability to secure financing. Additionally, it can increase the overall cost of the purchase and reduce the purchasing power of the dollar.

The City of New York has a number of exemptions and discounts for the Mansion Tax, which can help to minimize or even eliminate the amount that is owed. For example, first-time homebuyers who are purchasing their primary residence in the city can qualify for a full or partial exemption from the Mansion Tax.

Other exemptions and discounts are available for the purchase of a primary residence, second homes, and investment properties. These exemptions can be complex to navigate, and it is important to work with a knowledgeable real estate attorney or tax professional when applying for them. The city also needs to invest in education and enforcement of the Mansion Tax in order to ensure that all buyers and sellers comply with it. In addition, it may need to adjust the threshold for the Mansion Tax to account for inflation and rising Manhattan property values. 

Mansion Tax Thresholds in New York

The mansion tax is a real estate transfer tax that is levied when the purchase price of residential property exceeds $1 million. The tax is typically paid at closing.

The current statewide mansion tax is set at 1 percent of the sale price, while the New York City mansion tax increases on a sliding scale starting with residential properties that sell for $2 million, and it can reach 3.9 percent for sales over $25 million.

This tax is a significant impediment to high-end home buyers, and it can slow down the luxury real estate market. However, there are some legal ways that sellers and buyers can avoid or mitigate the impact of the mansion tax on a transaction.

Increasingly, buyers are seeking to buy homes that are just below the mansion tax threshold. In this way, they can circumvent the tax altogether by agreeing to a stated sale price that is less than $1 million, while negotiating an "under the table" payment for the amount of the real final sales price that is above the threshold. However, this is a serious breach of the law and can result in hefty fines for both the buyer and seller.

Another way to avoid the mansion tax is for a buyer to ask the seller to pay for it, or at least part of it. This is a common tactic, especially during times of strong demand for the luxury property market in NYC.

Some people even break the law and lie about their purchase price in order to avoid paying the mansion tax. This is a serious crime and can land both the buyer and seller in jail along with a large fine. However, there are still people who attempt to do this in spite of the risk.

Given the high price of Manhattan property, avoiding mansion tax isn't really an option for most buyers. The best strategy is to plan ahead and be aware of the implications of this tax when buying a home or condo in NYC. A real estate professional can help to guide buyers through the process. They can also recommend other ways to reduce or avoid the mansion tax based on your specific situation. 

Calculating Mansion Tax in New York

New York is infamous for its sky-high property tax rates and notoriously difficult closings. But what’s less well known is that it also has a mansion tax. And that, too, can add up to a sizable sum at closing time. For buyers of NYC real estate, the mansion tax is something to keep in mind when calculating your overall budget for a purchase.

The Mansion Tax Is a Statewide Tax

The mansion tax is a statewide transfer tax paid by the buyer of residential real property that meets certain criteria, including being priced over $1 million. It’s usually included in the total price of a purchase, but the specific amount varies depending on the sale price and whether or not you qualify for an exemption.

For example, if you buy a condo for $1.8 million, you will pay the standard 1% mansion tax. However, if you buy a new construction or resale home that is being sold for $2 million or more, you will pay an additional supplemental mansion tax of 1.2% of the sale price. The supplemental tax rate increases incrementally with the sale price of the property, topping out at 3.9% for properties sold at $25 million or more.

How Does the Mansion Tax Affect the Real Estate Market?

New York City’s mansion tax is a hefty supplemental transfer tax that has a major impact on the real estate market, especially in high-priced areas like Manhattan. The supplemental tax is often a deal-breaker for buyers of homes in these areas since it can push the sale price above the threshold for paying the 1% mansion tax.

Buyers should not try to dodge the mansion tax by agreeing to a published sales price below $1 million, as this is illegal. In fact, doing so may even result in a criminal investigation, so it’s definitely not worth it.
